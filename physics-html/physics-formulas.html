Physics Formulas and Units Lesson

Importance of Units in Calculations

Units are a fundamental aspect of calculations, though often overlooked as merely a way to earn marks. However, they play a crucial role in solving problems, especially in objective questions, in ways that might not be immediately apparent.

Consider the unit of speed, which is meters per second \( \text{ms}^{-1} \). From this unit, we can infer that speed is the distance covered divided by the time taken because \( \text{m} = \text{meters} \) and \( \text{s} = \text{seconds} \). Formulas can help derive units.
For example, in physics, the formula for Tensile stress is \( \frac{\text{Force}}{\text{Cross-sectional area}} \)
This tells us that the unit of Tensile stress is \( \text{Nm}^{-2} \) since force is \(\text{N (Newtons)}\) and area is \( \text{m}^2 \) and it is \( \frac{\text{Newtons}}{\text{meters}^2} \)

Speed and Velocity \( \text{ms}^{-1} \)

The formula for speed is \(\frac{distance}{time}\) while velocity is \(\frac{displacement}{time}\) since displacement and distance are in meters m and time is in seconds s.The unit of velocity and speed is \( \text{ms}^{-1} \) .
\(v\,=\,\frac{s}{t}\)

where \(s\,=\,\)distance and \(t\,=\,\)time

Acceleration \( \text{ms}^{-1} \)

The formula for acceleration is \(\frac{change\,in\,velocity}{time}\) . Change in velocity is final velocity minus initial velocity. It's unit is \(ms^{-1}\) and the unit of time is in seconds s therefore the unit of acceleration is \( \text{ms}^{-2} \) .
\(a\,=\,\frac{v\,-\,u}{t}\)

where \(v\,=\,\)final velocity, \(u\,=\,\) initial velocity and \(t\,=\,\)time taken

Force N

The formula for force is \(\text{mass} \times \text{acceleration}\). The unit of force is N (Newton).
\(F = m \times a\)

where \(F\,=\,\)force, \(m\,=\,\)mass, and \(a\,=\,\) acceleration

Work Done J

The formula for Work done is \(\text{force}\,\times\,\text{distance}\) The unit of Work done is Joules (J).
\(W\,=\,F\,\times\,s\)
\(W\,=\,mg\,\times\,s\)

where \(m\,=\,\)mass, \(a\,=\,\)acceleration, and \(s\,=\,\)distance

Kinetic Energy J

The formula for kinetic energy is \(\frac{1}{2} m v^2\). The unit of kinetic energy is Joules (J).
\(\text{KE} = \frac{1}{2} m v^2\)

where \(\text{KE}\,=\,\)kinetic energy, \(m\,=\,\)mass, and \(v\,=\,\)velocity

Potential Energy J

The formula for potential energy is \(mgh\). The unit of potential energy is Joules (J).
\(\text{PE} = mgh\)

where \(\text{PE}\,=\,\)potential energy, \(m\,= \,\) mass, \(g\,=\,\)acceleration due to gravity, and \(h \,=\,\) height

Tensile Stress \(Nm^{-2}\)

The formula for Tensile stress is \( \frac{\text{Force}}{\text{Cross-sectional area}} \) The unit of Tensile stress is \(Nm^{-2}\).
\(\text{Stress}\,=\,\frac{F}{A} \)
\(\text{Stress}\,=\,\frac{F}{\pi r^2}\)

where \(F\,=\,\)Force, \(A\,=\,\)Area, \(r\,=\,\)radius

Tensile Strain

The formula for strain is \(\frac{\text{extension}}{\text{original length}}\). Strain doesn't have a unit and this is because extension is in meters and length is also in meters and according to the formula it would mean meter over meter which would cancel out and leave no unit.
\(\text{Strain}\,=\,\frac{e}{l}\)

where \(e\,=\,\)extension and \(l\,=\,\)original length

Young's Modulus \(Nm^{-2}\)

The formula for Young's Modulus is \(\frac{\text{Stress}}{\text{Strain}}\). The unit for Young's Modulus is \(Nm^{-2}\). This is because Stress's units is \(Nm^{-2}\) and strain doesnt have a unit, meaning that It is \(Nm^{-2}\) over nothing.
\(\gamma\,=\,\frac{stress}{strain}\)

where \(\text{Stress}\,=\,\frac{\text{Force}}{\text{Area}}\) and \(\text{Strain}\,=\,\frac{\text{Extension}}{\text{Lenght}}\)

Linear Expansivity \(\text{K}^{-1}\)

The formula for linear expansivity is \(\frac{\Delta L}{L \Delta T}\). The unit of linear expansivity is \(\text{K}^{-1}\) that is \(K^{-1}\). Change in length and original length have the same units, so they cancel out and leave the unit of the change in temperature. For example, if the change in length and the original length are in meters and the change in temperature is in Kelvin, it would look something like this:

\(\frac{\text{m}}{\text{m} \times \text{K}}\)

The meters would cancel out, and we would be left with \(\frac{1}{K}\) i.e. \(K^{-1}\).
\(\alpha = \frac{\Delta L}{L \Delta T}\)

where \(\alpha\,=\,\)linear expansivity, \(\Delta L\,=\,\)change in length, \(L\, =\,\) original length, and \(\Delta T\,=\,\)change in temperature

Cubic Expansivity \(\text{K}^{-1}\)

The formula for cubic expansivity is \(\frac{\Delta V}{V \Delta T}\). The unit of cubic expansivity is \(\text{K}^{-1}\) that is \(K^{-1}\). Change in volume and original volume have the same units, so they cancel out and leave the unit of the change in temperature. For example, if the change in volume and the original volume are in cubic meters and the change in temperature is in Kelvin, it would look something like this:

\(\frac{\text{m}^3}{\text{m}^3 \times \text{K}}\)

The cubic meters would cancel out, and we would be left with \(\frac{1}{K}\) i.e. \(K^{-1}\).
\(\gamma = \frac{\Delta V}{V \Delta T}\)

where \(\gamma\,=\,\)cubic expansivity, \(\Delta V\,=\,\)change in volume, \(V\,=\,\)original volume, and \(\Delta T\,=\,\)change in temperature

Apparent Volume Expansivity \(\text{K}^{-1}\)

The formula for apparent volume expansivity is \(\frac{\Delta V_a}{V \Delta T}\). The unit of apparent volume expansivity is \(\text{K}^{-1}\) that is \(K^{-1}\). Change in volume and original volume have the same units, so they cancel out and leave the unit of the change in temperature. For example, if the change in volume and the original volume are in cubic meters and the change in temperature is in Kelvin, it would look something like this:

\(\frac{\text{m}^3}{\text{m}^3 \times \text{K}}\)

The cubic meters would cancel out, and we would be left with \(\frac{1}{K}\) i.e. \(K^{-1}\).
\(\beta_a = \frac{\Delta V_a}{V \Delta T}\)

where \(\beta_a\,=\,\)apparent volume expansivity, \(\Delta V_a\,=\,\)apparent change in volume \(V\,=\,\)original volume, and \(\Delta T \,=\,\)change in temperature

Real Cubic Expansivity \(\text{K}^{-1}\)

The formula for real cubic expansivity is \(\frac{\Delta V_r}{V \Delta T}\). The unit of real cubic expansivity is \(\text{K}^{-1}\) that is \(K^{-1}\). Change in volume and original volume have the same units, so they cancel out and leave the unit of the change in temperature. For example, if the change in volume and the original volume are in cubic meters and the change in temperature is in Kelvin, it would look something like this:

\(\frac{\text{m}^3}{\text{m}^3 \times \text{K}}\)

The cubic meters would cancel out, and we would be left with \(\frac{1}{K}\) i.e. \(K^{-1}\).
\(\gamma_r = \frac{\Delta V_r}{V \Delta T}\)

where \(\gamma_r\,=\,\)real cubic expansivity, \(\Delta V_r\,=\,\) real change in volume, \(V\,=\,\)original volume, and \(\Delta T\,=\,\)change in temperature

Power W

The formula for power is \(\frac{\text{work done}}{\text{time}}\) or \(\text{force} \times \text{velocity}\). The unit of power is Watts (W).
\(P = \frac{W}{t}\)
\(P = F \times v\)

where \(P\,=\,\)power, \(W\,=\,\)work done, \(t\,= \,\)time, \(F\,=\,\)force, and \(v\,=\,\)velocity

Efficiency %

The formula for efficiency is \(\frac{\text{useful energy output}}{\text{total energy input}} \times 100\) or \(\frac{\text{useful power output}}{\text{total power input}} \times 100\). The unit of efficiency is percentage %.
\(\eta = \frac{E_{\text{out}}}{E_{\text{in}}} \times 100\)
\(\eta = \frac{P_{\text{out}}}{P_{\text{in}}} \times 100\)

where \(\eta\,=\,\)efficiency, \(E_{\text{out}} \,= \,\)useful energy output, \(E_{\text{in}}\,= \,\)total energy input, \(P_{\text{out}}\,= \,\)useful power output, and \(P_{\text{in}}\,= \,\)total power input

Voltage V

The formula for voltage is \(\frac{\text{work done}}{\text{charge}}\) or \(\text{current} \times \text{resistance}\). The unit of voltage is Volts (V).
\(V = \frac{W}{Q}\)
\(V = I \times R\)

where \(V\,=\,\)voltage, \(W\,=\,\)work done, \(Q\,=\,\)charge, \(I\,=\,\)current, and \(R\,=\,\)resistance

Current A

The formula for current is \(\frac{\text{charge}}{\text{time}}\) or \(\frac{\text{voltage}}{\text{resistance}}\). The unit of current is Amperes (A).
\(I = \frac{Q}{t}\)
\(I = \frac{V}{R}\)

where \(I \,=\,\)current, \(Q \,=\,\)charge, \(t\,= \,\)time, \(V \,=\,\)voltage, and \(R \,= \,\)resistance

Resistance Ω

The formula for resistance is \(\frac{\text{voltage}}{\text{current}}\). The unit of resistance is Ohms (Ω).
\(R = \frac{V}{I}\)

where \(R \,=\,\)resistance, \(V\, =\,\)voltage, and \(I \,=\,\)current

Resistance in Parallel Ω

The formula for the total resistance of resistors in parallel is \(\frac{1}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{R_n}\). The unit of resistance is Ohms (Ω).
\(\frac{1}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{R_n}\)

where \(R_{\text{total}} \,=\,\)total resistance, and \(R_1, R_2, \ldots, R_n\, = \,\)individual resistances

Resistance in Series Ω

The formula for the total resistance of resistors in series is \(\text{R}_{\text{total}} = R_1 + R_2 + \cdots + R_n\). The unit of resistance is Ohms (Ω).
\(R_{\text{total}} = R_1 + R_2 + \cdots + R_n\)

where \(R_{\text{total}} \,=\,\)total resistance, and \(R_1, R_2, \ldots, R_n\, = \,\)individual resistances

Electrical Energy J

The formula for electrical energy is \(\text{power} \times \text{time}\), or \(\text{voltage} \times \text{current} \times \text{time}\), or \(\frac{1}{2} \times\) \(\text{current}^2 \times\) \(\text{resistance} \times\) \(\text{time}\), or \(\text{voltage}^2 \times \frac{\text{time}}{\text{resistance}}\). The unit of electrical energy is Joules (J).
\(E = P \times t\)
\(E = V \times I \times t\)
\(E = \frac{1}{2} I^2 R t\)
\(E = \frac{V^2 t}{R}\)

where \(E = \)electrical energy, \(P \,=\,\)power, \(t \,=\,\)time, \(V \,= \,\)voltage, \(I \,=\,\) current, \(R \,=\, \)resistance

Electrical Power W

The formula for electrical power is \(\text{voltage} \times \text{current}\), or \(\frac{\text{electrical energy}}{\text{time}}\), or \(\text{current}^2 \times \text{resistance}\), or \(\frac{\text{voltage}^2}{\text{resistance}}\). The unit of electrical power is Watts (W).
\(P = V \times I\)
\(P = \frac{E}{t}\)
\(P = I^2 R\)
\(P = \frac{V^2}{R}\)

where \(P \,= \,\)power, \(V\,= \,\)voltage, \(I\,= \,\)current, \(\,= \,\)electrical energy, \(t\,= \,\)time, and \(R\,= \,\)resistance
