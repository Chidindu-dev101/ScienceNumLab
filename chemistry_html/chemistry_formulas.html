Chemistry Formula and Units Lesson

Importance of Units in Calculations

Units are a fundamental aspect of calculations, though often overlooked as merely a way to earn marks. However, they play a crucial role in solving problems, especially in objective questions, in ways that might not be immediately apparent.

In chemistry, formulas also help derive units. For example, the formula for molar concentration is \(\frac{\text{Amount of Substance in moles}}{\text{Volume in } \text{dm}^3}\) . The unit of volume is \( \text{dm}^3 \) and the unit for Amount of Substance in moles is \( \text{mol} \) . This tells us that the unit of molar concentration is \( \text{mol} \text{dm}^{-3} \) .

Mole Concept

The formula for the mole concept is \(n = \frac{m}{M}\). The unit of the mole is mol. Here, \(m\) is the mass in grams and \(M\) is the molar mass in grams per mole.
\(n = \frac{m}{M}\)

where \(n\) is the number of moles.

Concentration of Solutions

The formula for concentration of a solution is \(C = \frac{n}{V}\). The unit of concentration is mol/L or M.
\(C = \frac{n}{V}\)

where \(C\) is concentration, \(n\) is the number of moles, and \(V\) is the volume in liters.

Ideal Gas Law

The formula for the ideal gas law is \(PV = nRT\). The units of the variables are: pressure \(P\) in pascals (Pa), volume \(V\) in cubic meters (m³), number of moles \(n\) in moles (mol), gas constant \(R\) in J/(mol·K), and temperature \(T\) in kelvin (K).
\(PV = nRT\)

This equation relates the pressure, volume, and temperature of an ideal gas.

Acid-Base Reactions

The formula for an acid-base reaction is \(\text{Acid} + \text{Base} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{Water}\).
\(\text{Acid} + \text{Base} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{Water}\)

This is a general representation of an acid-base neutralization reaction.

Redox Reactions

The general form of a redox reaction is \(\text{Oxidation} + \text{Reduction}\).
\(\text{Oxidation} + \text{Reduction}\)

where oxidation is the loss of electrons and reduction is the gain of electrons.

Equilibrium Constant (Kc)

The formula for the equilibrium constant is \(K_c = \frac{[C]^c [D]^d}{[A]^a [B]^b}\). The unit of the equilibrium constant depends on the balanced chemical equation.
\(K_c = \frac{[C]^c [D]^d}{[A]^a [B]^b}\)

where \([A]\), \([B]\), \([C]\), and \([D]\) are the concentrations of the reactants and products.

Rate of Reaction

The formula for the rate of reaction is \(\text{Rate} = k[A]^m[B]^n\). The unit of the rate depends on the order of the reaction.
\(\text{Rate} = k[A]^m[B]^n\)

where \(k\) is the rate constant, \([A]\) and \([B]\) are the concentrations, and \(m\) and \(n\) are the orders of the reaction.

pH and pOH

The formulas for pH and pOH are \(\text{pH} = -\log[H^+]\) and \(\text{pOH} = -\log[OH^-]\).
\(\text{pH} = -\log[H^+]\)
\(\text{pOH} = -\log[OH^-]\)

These formulas relate the concentrations of hydrogen and hydroxide ions to their respective pH and pOH values.

Enthalpy Change (ΔH)

The formula for enthalpy change is \(ΔH = H_{\text{products}} - H_{\text{reactants}}\). The unit of enthalpy change is kJ/mol.
\(ΔH = H_{\text{products}} - H_{\text{reactants}}\)

This equation represents the heat change during a chemical reaction at constant pressure.

Entropy (ΔS)

The formula for entropy change is \(ΔS = S_{\text{products}} - S_{\text{reactants}}\). The unit of entropy change is J/(mol·K).
\(ΔS = S_{\text{products}} - S_{\text{reactants}}\)

This equation represents the change in disorder or randomness during a chemical reaction.

Gibbs Free Energy (ΔG)

The formula for Gibbs free energy change is \(ΔG = ΔH - TΔS\). The unit of Gibbs free energy change is kJ/mol.
\(ΔG = ΔH - TΔS\)

This equation combines enthalpy, entropy, and temperature to determine the spontaneity of a reaction.

Boyle's Law

The formula for Boyle's Law is \(P_1V_1 = P_2V_2\). The units of pressure and volume are typically atm (atmospheres) and L (liters), respectively.
\(P_1V_1 = P_2V_2\)

This equation shows the inverse relationship between pressure and volume at constant temperature.

Charles's Law

The formula for Charles's Law is \(\frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2}\). The units of volume and temperature are typically L (liters) and K (kelvin), respectively.
\(\frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2}\)

This equation shows the direct relationship between volume and temperature at constant pressure.

Avogadro's Law

The formula for Avogadro's Law is \(\frac{V_1}{n_1} = \frac{V_2}{n_2}\). The units of volume and amount of substance are typically L (liters) and mol (moles), respectively.
\(\frac{V_1}{n_1} = \frac{V_2}{n_2}\)

This equation shows the direct relationship between volume and amount of gas at constant temperature and pressure.

Electrolysis

The formula for the amount of substance produced or consumed during electrolysis is \(n = \frac{It}{F}\). The units of current, time, and Faraday's constant are A (amperes), s (seconds), and C/mol (coulombs per mole), respectively.
\(n = \frac{It}{F}\)

where \(I\) is the current, \(t\) is the time, and \(F\) is Faraday's constant (96,485 C/mol).

Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

The first law of electrolysis is \(m = \frac{Q}{F} \times M\), and the second law is \(m = \frac{It}{F} \times M\).
\(m = \frac{Q}{F} \times M\)
\(m = \frac{It}{F} \times M\)

where \(m\) is the mass of the substance, \(Q\) is the charge, \(I\) is the current, \(t\) is the time, \(F\) is Faraday's constant, and \(M\) is the molar mass.

Graham's Law of Effusion

The formula for Graham's Law is \(\frac{r_1}{r_2} = \sqrt{\frac{M_2}{M_1}}\). The units of rate of effusion and molar mass are typically given as L/s (liters per second) and g/mol (grams per mole), respectively.
\(\frac{r_1}{r_2} = \sqrt{\frac{M_2}{M_1}}\)

This equation shows the relationship between the rates of effusion of two gases and their molar masses.

Hess's Law

Hess's Law states that the enthalpy change of a reaction is the same, no matter the pathway taken. The formula is \(ΔH_{\text{total}} = ΣΔH_{\text{steps}}\).
\(ΔH_{\text{total}} = ΣΔH_{\text{steps}}\)

This law allows the calculation of enthalpy changes for complex reactions.

Le Chatelier's Principle

Le Chatelier's Principle states that if a dynamic equilibrium is disturbed, the position of equilibrium moves to counteract the change.
\(\text{Equilibrium shift}\)

Changes in concentration, temperature, or pressure will shift the equilibrium position.

Nernst Equation

The Nernst equation is \(E = E^0 - \frac{RT}{nF} \ln Q\). The units of potential, gas constant, temperature, and Faraday's constant are V (volts), J/(mol·K), K (kelvin), and C/mol (coulombs per mole), respectively.
\(E = E^0 - \frac{RT}{nF} \ln Q\)

This equation relates the reduction potential of a cell to the standard electrode potential, temperature, and reaction quotient.

Rate Law

The general rate law is \(Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n\). The units of rate constant \(k\) depend on the order of the reaction.
\(Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n\)

where \(k\) is the rate constant, \([A]\) and \([B]\) are the concentrations of reactants, and \(m\) and \(n\) are their respective reaction orders.

Solubility Product Constant (Ksp)

The solubility product constant is \(Ksp = [A^+][B^-]\). The units depend on the ions involved in the equilibrium.
\(Ksp = [A^+][B^-]\)

This equation represents the product of the molar concentrations of the ions, each raised to the power of their coefficients in the balanced equation.

Van der Waals Equation

The Van der Waals equation is \( \left( P + \frac{a}{V^2} \right) (V - b) = RT \). The units of pressure, volume, gas constant, and temperature are typically atm (atmospheres), L (liters), L·atm/(mol·K), and K (kelvin), respectively.
\( \left( P + \frac{a}{V^2} \right) (V - b) = RT \)

This equation accounts for the non-ideal behavior of gases, where \(a\) and \(b\) are Van der Waals constants.

Half-Life (Radioactive Decay)

The formula for half-life is \( t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} \). The unit of decay constant (\(\lambda\)) is typically s\(^{-1}\) (per second).
\( t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} \)

where \( t_{1/2} \) is the half-life and \( \lambda \) is the decay constant.

Osmotic Pressure

The formula for osmotic pressure is \( \Pi = iMRT \). The units of osmotic pressure, molarity, gas constant, and temperature are typically atm (atmospheres), mol/L (molarity), L·atm/(mol·K), and K (kelvin), respectively.
\( \Pi = iMRT \)

where \( \Pi \) is the osmotic pressure, \( i \) is the van't Hoff factor, \( M \) is the molarity, \( R \) is the gas constant, and \( T \) is the temperature.

Arrhenius Equation

The formula for the Arrhenius equation is \( k = A e^{-\frac{E_a}{RT}} \). The units of the rate constant, frequency factor, activation energy, gas constant, and temperature are typically s\(^{-1}\) (per second), varies, J/mol (joules per mole), J/(mol·K), and K (kelvin), respectively.
\( k = A e^{-\frac{E_a}{RT}} \)

where \( k \) is the rate constant, \( A \) is the frequency factor, \( E_a \) is the activation energy, \( R \) is the gas constant, and \( T \) is the temperature.
